## Story Summary
Abel Mauser, a recently laid-off late 20-something, is invited by the only online ad he could find promising on-the-job training to a remote Maine fishing village. He is tasked with an increasingly difficult (and dangerous) series of fishing assignments, including catching increasingly bizarre fish that attack his boat. 

Despite the danger, he feels a "sense of ownership" over his work and it helps him not think of the future. Should he (and the player) decide to be more curious and thorough, they can discover the truth about the town: abandoned in the 50s, the community was offered to make an occasional sacrifice to a "deep one" that lives in an adjacent, unnaturally formed deep sea sinkhole to keep the town's denizens immortal yet infertile, their style and mannerisms trapped in the style of the mid 20th century.

Some eerie occurrences happen during his stay. During a fish boil in the center of town, he imagines he sees the odd fish twitching while being cooked, as if still alive (which would be impossible). NPCs have strange dialog relating to "brining in fresh blood" to the town and making constant archaic pop culture references which feels out of place. The town also sports strange architecture and jewelry on citizens which Abel struggles to understand.

There are 3 main endings being considered. The first is where you ultimately get sacrificed to the deep one because you didn't achieve the ending criteria - getting yanked off your ship by a tentacle out of the water. The good ending is that you accept the job but learned about the intention, and have fully upgraded your sonar equipment, including a custom upgrade that requires talking with your friend on the phone about. Doing this will let you use the sonar to repel and ultimately put back to sleep the deep one and escape on the boat, only to get picked up by the coast guard who thankfully let you keep your absconded boat.

What could be considered an "ending" is you getting fired from failing to make the fishing grade. While you avoid possibly drowning/dying, it's implied you'll continue and will in fact sink deeper into depression due to failing to hold down a job successfully.

## Ludonarrative Guidelines
*~ Or: what game design principles should we adopt given the nature of the narrative ~*

##### Principle 1: \"Horror on the periphery\"

The town *wants* you to keep fishing. You'll be treated quite well by those designated to maintaining your stay there. This includes the 3 main characters (the "handlers") of Walsh, Brad, and Jeffrey. However, exploring areas *off* of what's strictly necessary to progress will reveal increasingly odd dialogue. Townsfolk NPCs will either primarily have startled, incongruous, or vaguely hostile responses to your questions.  

The danger you're being led to will be treated as normal occupational hazards by the town, implicitly playing to Abel's sense of masculinity to keep pushing. He (and you) will continue to push out of pride, but dramatic irony dictates that you as the player also know you're walking into a sacrificial trap. Perhaps you as a player might also represent part of his brain that feels the same way???

**Principle 2: Let sounds do the heavy lifting**
Horror will be achieved primarily through the use of **implication** and **sound**. **If we successfully get the player's brain to go into 'what the fuck was that' mode through sight and sound, we have succeeded in the main thrust of the game jam**
